Data basics

Tables, rows & columns

Row = Observation
One record of data — a single Taylor Swift song, one customer transaction, one survey response.
Column = Variable
A measured attribute — song name, danceability score, album year. Also called a dimension or measure.
Shape = (rows, cols)
The Taylor Swift Spotify dataset is shape (73, 14) — 73 songs × 14 attributes. Always check shape first.
Key terminology
Dimension — qualitative value (name, date, region) Measure — numeric, quantitative value Metadata — data about data
Data types

Qualitative vs Quantitative

Qualitative — Nominal
No natural order
Examples: album name, genre, country, eye colour
Order doesn't matter — "Pop" is not greater than "Rock"
Qualitative — Ordinal
Has meaningful order
Examples: star rating, satisfaction level, grade (A/B/C)
Order matters — "Very satisfied" > "Satisfied" but gap is undefined
Quantitative — Discrete
Countable whole numbers
Examples: number of streams, tracks on album, listens today
You can count it — no fractions (can't have 2.7 songs)
Quantitative — Continuous
Measurable, infinite values
Examples: danceability (0.0–1.0), tempo, loudness, duration
You measure it — any value in a range is possible

Descriptive statistics

Central tendency & dispersion

  • μ
    Mean — the average
    Sum all values ÷ count. Sensitive to outliers — one $1M salary pulls the mean up for a team earning $50K.
  • M
    Median — the middle value
    Sort data, find the midpoint. Robust to outliers — use for skewed distributions like income, house prices.
  • σ²
    Variance — average squared deviation from mean
    Σ(x − μ)² ÷ N. Tells you how spread out data is. Always non-negative.
  • σ
    Standard deviation — square root of variance
    Back in original units. 68% of data falls within 1σ of the mean in a normal distribution.

Data preparation

Data cleaning — always first

GIGO: Garbage In, Garbage Out. The quality of your analysis = the quality of your data.

1
Remove irrelevant data
Drop columns outside scope — e.g. "Favourite Sports Team" in a customer satisfaction study
2
Remove duplicates
Identical customer entries, duplicate invoices, repeated social media logs
3
Fix structural errors
"NY" vs "New York", height in inches AND cm, misaligned columns from import
4
Handle outliers
$1M salary in a $50K dataset. Age of 150. Temperature spike of 200°F. Decide: remove or investigate?
5
Type conversion
"100" stored as text, date formats inconsistent, Yes/No vs True/False booleans
6
Handle missing values
Blank cells, NULL/NaN/N/A fields, skipped survey responses — impute or drop?

Visualization — the how

Right chart for the right message

You want to show how monthly revenue changed over a year.
You want to compare sales across 5 product categories.
You want to show the distribution of ages in a dataset (continuous).
Common confusion

Bar chart vs histogram

Bar chart
Discrete categories. Gaps between bars are intentional — they signal the X-axis is categorical, not continuous. Use for: comparing regions, products, groups.
Histogram
Continuous data. Bars touch — there's no gap because the data is one continuous range divided into bins. Use for: age distribution, test scores, danceability.
Visualization best practices

Gestalt principles

Enclosure
A border or boundary groups elements — they belong together because they share a container.
Similarity
Objects that look alike are perceived as a group — color, shape, or size communicates category.
Proximity
Elements close together are seen as related. Distance implies separation and difference.
Continuity
Eyes follow lines and curves — points on a line feel connected even without explicit connection.
Figure / Ground
We distinguish objects from their background — contrast creates hierarchy and draws attention.
Common fate
Elements moving or pointing in the same direction are perceived as a group or trend.
Best practices — axis debate

Start axis at zero?

Darrell Huff — "How to Lie with Statistics"
Always start at zero.
Starting anywhere but zero is a means of being deceptive. A truncated axis exaggerates changes, making small differences appear dramatic and misleading readers about scale.
vs
Edward Tufte — "The Visual Display of Quantitative Information"
Show the data, not empty space.
"Context does not come from empty vertical space reaching down to zero." Show more data horizontally for context. For time-series, use a baseline that shows the data — not the zero point.
Practical rule: Start at zero for bar charts (comparing absolute quantities). Consider truncating for time-series line charts showing small changes in already-large numbers — but label the axis clearly.
Visualization — color

Color encodes meaning

Sequential
One hue, varying intensity. Use for ordered data — population density, temperature, revenue. Light = low, dark = high.
Diverging
Two hues from a neutral midpoint. Use when data has a meaningful center — profit/loss, above/below average, positive/negative sentiment.
Categorical
Distinct hues, equal visual weight. Use for unordered groups — regions, product lines, team names. Max 6–7 colors.
Golden rule
Color should encode meaning, not decoration. One accent color draws attention. Never rely on color alone — add labels, patterns, or shapes for accessibility.
Two modes of visualization

Exploratory vs Explanatory

Exploratory
Visualizations for me
  • → Fast and messy is fine
  • → No audience except yourself
  • → Purpose: find patterns, outliers, hypotheses
  • → Tools: EDA, scatter matrices, quick histograms
  • → Can be complex and dense
Explanatory
Visualizations for them
  • → Polished and clear is essential
  • → Designed for a specific audience
  • → Purpose: communicate one clear insight
  • → Tools: dashboards, presentations, reports
  • → Remove everything that doesn't serve the story
Visualizations are the vehicles of your story — but the chart should complement the narrative, not overwhelm it.
Best practices

Declutter checklist

❌ Avoid these
  • Diagonal text labels (rotate instead)
  • 3D chart effects — distort perception
  • More than 6-7 colors in one chart
  • Gridlines darker than the data
  • Pie charts with more than 4-5 slices
  • Unlabelled axes or missing units
✓ Best practices
  • Left-align text for readability
  • Direct labels instead of a legend
  • Consistent color across related charts
  • Highlight only the key insight
  • One chart = one message
  • Always cite your data source
Reduce cognitive load
Every element a reader has to decode is a tax on their attention. Remove anything that doesn't contribute to the central message. The best chart is the simplest one that tells the story.
Storytelling with data

The three-part data story

01
Beginning — Why
Define the clear objective. What is the question or problem? Who is the audience? What decision must they make after seeing this?
→
02
Middle — What
Present key insights with supporting visualizations. Build context, show the data, surface the pattern. Logical, engaging order.
→
03
End — So what
Actionable takeaway. What should the audience do now? Recommend a decision, propose next steps, or deepen understanding.
"The goal is not to show all the data. The goal is to show the insight."
Critical thinking

Correlation ≠ Causation

True correlation
Height and shoe size. Taller people genuinely tend to have larger feet — a real biological relationship with a plausible mechanism.
Spurious correlation
Ice cream sales and drowning. Both rise in summer — but temperature is the lurking variable driving both. Ice cream does not cause drowning.
No correlation
Movies released per year and earthquake frequency. These are unrelated — any apparent pattern is random noise in the data.
Questions to ask before claiming causation: Is there a plausible mechanism? Was it a controlled experiment? Could a lurking variable explain both? Was it replicated across different datasets?
