Bridge from bootcamp

Correlation → Regression

Correlation tells you…
Direction and strength of the relationship between X and Y. r = 0.85 means strong positive. But it gives you no line, no formula, no prediction.
Regression gives you…
The actual equation of the line. You can plug in any X and get a predicted Y. Correlation is the "is there a relationship?" — regression is the "what exactly is it?"
Slope formula (closed form)
w = Cov(X,Y) / Var(X)

w = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²
The special cases
Feature constant → w undefined (Var(X) = 0)
Target constant → w = 0 (Cov = 0)
Both vary → w is meaningful
The model

ŷ = wx + b

prediction = weight × feature + bias
📐
w — the weight (slope)
For every 1 unit increase in X, ŷ increases by w units. Controls the tilt of the line.
w = 3 → score rises 3 pts per hour
🎯
b — the bias (intercept)
The value of ŷ when X = 0. Shifts the entire line up or down. Learned alongside w.
b = 40 → baseline score is 40
🔮
ŷ — the prediction
Plug in any X to get the predicted Y. The goal of the whole model — not the actual Y, the best estimate.
x=5 → ŷ = 3×5+40 = 55

Error — loss function

How do we measure mistake?

Step 1 — residual error
errorᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ
Actual minus predicted. Positive = we under-predicted. Negative = we over-predicted.
Step 2 — why square?
errorᵢ² = (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
Makes negatives positive. Penalises big errors more than small ones (non-linear punishment).
Step 3 — SSE
SSE = Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
Sum over ALL observations. One number that captures total badness. Minimise this → best line.
SSE = 0
Perfect fit. Every prediction exactly right. Rare in real data — means zero noise.
SSE is large
Line is far from the data. w and b need a lot of updating before the model is useful.
SSE at minimum
Best possible fit with a straight line. Some SSE may remain — that's irreducible data noise.

Why not just sum errors?
+2 and −2 cancel to 0, hiding the fact we made mistakes on both sides.
Why square?
−2 becomes 4. Errors become positive AND large errors are punished more heavily.
The goal
Find w and b that make this total as small as possible — that's the optimal line.
Calculus — intuition first

Why do we differentiate?

SSE is a bowl-shaped curve
If you plot SSE against w, you get a parabola. There is exactly one minimum — the lowest point of the bowl. That's the optimal w.
At the minimum, slope = 0
At the very bottom of any bowl, the curve is flat — neither rising nor falling. Flat means the derivative equals zero.
So we set ∂SSE/∂w = 0
Solve that equation for w and you get the exact formula: w = Cov(X,Y)/Var(X). The calculus collapses into one clean answer.
Calculus — chain rule

The chain rule, plain English

SSE = (y − wx − b)² has two layers nested inside each other. You can't differentiate just the outer layer.

Outer function
f(u) = u²
df/du = 2u
×
Inner function
u = y−wx−b
du/dw = −x
=
Chain rule result
2u × (−x)
= −2x(y−ŷ)
Why 2u? (power rule)
d(u²)/du = 2u. Bring the exponent down, reduce it by 1. Standard calculus power rule.
Why −x? (inner derivative)
u = y − wx − b. Only −wx has w. Its derivative w.r.t. w is just its coefficient: −x.
Gear analogy
Wheel/hand = (wheel/gear) × (gear/hand). Two rates multiply through layers. That's the chain rule.
Calculus — gradients

∂SSE/∂w and ∂SSE/∂b

Gradient of w
∂SSE/∂w = −2Σ xᵢ(yᵢ−ŷᵢ)
The xᵢ multiplier is there because w controls the tilt — its effect on SSE is amplified by how far each point is along the x-axis. Distant points feel w changes more.
Gradient of b
∂SSE/∂b = −2Σ (yᵢ−ŷᵢ)
No xᵢ here. b shifts the entire line up or down equally for all x — so its gradient only cares about the raw sum of errors, not where each point sits.
What the sign tells you
Gradient positive → SSE rises as w increases → decrease w
Gradient negative → SSE falls as w increases → increase w
Gradient = 0 → at the minimum → stop
Key difference
w's gradient amplifies by xᵢ — distant points pull w harder. b's gradient treats every point equally — it only cares about average residual.

Update rules

How w and b update each iteration

Update w
w ← w − lr × ∂SSE/∂w
w ← w − lr × (−2Σ xᵢeᵢ)
lr × gradient tells us how far to step. The minus sign means we always step downhill — opposite the direction SSE is climbing.
Update b
b ← b − lr × ∂SSE/∂b
b ← b − lr × (−2Σ eᵢ)
Same structure as w. b has no xᵢ because shifting the whole line up/down affects every point equally, regardless of where they sit on the x-axis.
Learning rate too large
Steps overshoot the minimum. SSE bounces around instead of converging smoothly. Model diverges.
lr = 0.5 → SSE oscillates
Learning rate too small
Tiny steps converge very slowly. Model is correct eventually, but takes thousands of iterations.
lr = 0.00001 → slow to learn

After each update, go back to step 1: predict → compute SSE → compute gradients → update. Repeat until SSE stops falling.

Convergence

How the model "learns"

  • 1
    Initialise
    Start with random guesses for w and b (e.g. w=1, b=5). The line is far from the data — SSE is huge.
  • 2
    Predict
    For every observation, compute ŷ = wx + b using current w and b.
  • 3
    Compute SSE
    Sum all (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)². One number capturing total error. This is what we want to minimise.
  • 4
    Compute gradients
    ∂SSE/∂w and ∂SSE/∂b — which direction is SSE climbing? How steeply?
  • 5
    Update w and b
    Step downhill: w ← w − lr×grad_w, b ← b − lr×grad_b. Both update simultaneously.
  • →
    Repeat from step 2
    Keep iterating. SSE falls fast at first (large gradients), then slows near the minimum (small gradients). Stop when SSE barely changes.
